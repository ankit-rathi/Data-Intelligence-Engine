# PART 1 — Decisions, Uncertainty & Value (The Core)

## 1D. Metrics vs Reality — When KPIs Lie

**Next Topic: 1E. Correlation, Causation & False Confidence**

---

# RIGHT PAGE — ARTICLE (≈382 words)

Metrics are proxies.

Reality is the underlying system that generates outcomes.

Confusing the two is one of the most expensive mistakes organizations make.

A KPI does not measure value. It measures a variable assumed to correlate with value. That assumption is a structural bet. When the proxy diverges from the underlying economic driver, decisions become misaligned.

This is not a statistical problem. It is an incentive problem.

When performance evaluation, compensation, and reputation are tied to metrics, people optimize the metric. Over time, effort shifts from improving the underlying system to improving the number. The proxy becomes the target.

Once that happens, the metric stops being informative.

Consider customer acquisition targets. If teams are rewarded purely on new sign-ups, they will maximize sign-ups — even if retention collapses. The KPI rises. Enterprise value falls. The proxy has decoupled from reality.

This divergence occurs through three mechanisms.

First, simplification. Metrics compress multidimensional reality into single numbers. Compression hides tradeoffs.

Second, gaming. Rational actors respond to incentives. If a metric defines success, behavior will adapt around its blind spots.

Third, lag. Many KPIs are lagging indicators. By the time the metric reflects damage, structural decay has already compounded.

The cost is misallocated capital. Organizations invest in activities that inflate metrics instead of improving future cash flows. The dashboard shows progress. The economics deteriorate.

The solution is not abandoning metrics. It is treating them as instruments, not truth.

Every KPI should answer two questions:

What causal mechanism links this number to future value creation?
Under what conditions could this linkage break?

If leadership cannot articulate the mechanism, the KPI is decorative.
If it cannot identify failure modes, the KPI is fragile.

Metrics are useful only insofar as they preserve alignment between measurement and economic reality.

When proxies drift, decision quality collapses — quietly.

Next, we examine how correlation without causation amplifies false confidence, creating the illusion of understanding where none exists.

A KPI is not reality.
It’s a proxy for reality.

And every proxy is an assumption about what truly drives value.

The problem starts when incentives attach to the proxy.
People optimize the number, not the underlying system.

If you reward sign-ups, you’ll get sign-ups.
Retention, quality, and long-term cash flow might quietly decline.

This is called metric drift. The proxy decouples from economic reality.

Metrics compress complexity into single numbers. That hides tradeoffs.
Most KPIs are also lagging indicators. By the time they show damage, the damage has compounded.

So the discipline is simple:

Can you clearly explain how this metric causally links to future cash flows?
And under what conditions that link might break?

If you can’t answer those questions, the metric isn’t guiding decisions.
It’s creating false confidence.

Strong systems treat KPIs as instruments — not truth.
